The phonon density of states (DOS) originates with interatomic forces, and we have developed computational methods to extract the interatomic force constants from the measured inelastic scattering data, termed "Born-von Karman inversions." An added bonus of these procedures is their ability to correct experimental DOS curves for "neutron weighting," where phonon modes with large displacements of elements with big phonon cross-sections tend to be over-emphasized. A Born-von Karman model of lattice dynamics was developed for several metal alloys, where forces between atom pairs were represented by pairs of tensorial force constants that were optimized iteratively. Fits to experimental data were performed using the known neutron weightings of the elements, the instrument resolution function, and errors from experimental counting statistics.


While the phonon entropy of solids is known to depend on chemical composition and structure at low temperatures, we have performed experiments to addresses the importance of electron-phonon interactions at higher temperatures. It is the free energy at elevated temperatures that is relevant for phase diagrams of most engineering materials. Temperature presents no new phenomena for configurational entropy, which depends only on atom configurations. The situation is more complicated for the vibrational entropy. From what we know so far, the phonon DOS usually changes with temperature, and usually these changes have thermodynamic importance. We also know, of course, that thermal expansion occurs because the interatomic potentials are not quadratic with atomic displacements. Larger lattice parameters weaken the interatomic forces, lower the vibrational frequencies, and increase the vibrational entropy. This increased vibrational entropy competes with an increased elastic energy of expansion in the free energy F = U – TS. The minimum of F therefore occurs for a larger lattice parameter at higher temperatures. In addition to pure phonon excitations, electron-phonon interactions can also contribute to anharmonic behavior. For the thermal excitation of electron-phonon and phonon-phonon interactions, there is a naive expectation that their contributions to phonon anharmonicity will scale approximately as the Fermi temperature to the Debye temperature, minimizing the importance of electron-phonon interactions. This is not so clear for transition metals, however, where electron-phonon interactions cause Kohn anomalies. These two sources of anharmonicity, electron-phonon and phonon-phonon, are difficult to separate experimentally, and thermodynamic analyses usually group them together. We have performed Raman scattering measurements on several transition metal oxides at a series of elevated temperatures in order to better discern the origin of the phonon anharmonicity found in these materials.

The weaker 2nd Stokes shift is used as a probe beam while the 1st Stokes provides the pumping to the electronic excited state. A variable time delay provides access to vibrational dynamics. A material in an excited state is essentially a different material, with its own phonon thermodynamics. Reliable experimental results on such systems will enable important tests of theoretical predictions of electronic structure beyond the ground state. We design experiments for materials where the phonon vibrational periods are much faster than the characteristic electronic transition time, allowing separation of electronic and vibrational degrees of freedom. We will perform our first studies with time-resolved Raman spectroscopy at Caltech, and later move to neutron scattering experiments when instrumentation at the SNS is available.


The study of materials and molecules with electrons in excited states is emerging as new field of research. During the lifetimes of excited states, which can be milliseconds or even longer, it is now possible to measure the structures of atom arrangements and how they change with the excitation. Structural distortions are known to couple to the excitations, as in the Marcus theory of chemical reaction rates, for example. On the other hand, research into the dynamics of atom motions in excited systems remains in its infancy. Atom dynamics, such as phonons in solids, have proved rigorous tests of electronic structure calculations with density functional methods, and the systematics of these ab-initio phonon calculations are now becoming understood. The electronic structure of excited systems requires different computational methods, and progress on these methods is expected to be rapid over the next decade. Dynamics studies are again expected to provide incisive tests of the computational methods for materials in excited states.


Electronic excited states are commonly discussed in materials chemistry, and are often associated with "nonadiabatic processes" where electron transfer occurs during chemical reactions. Examples include the interaction between alkali and halogen atoms as a step in forming an ionic solid, and some surface oxidation processes such as the sticking of the O2 molecule on an Al (111) surface. Progress has been made in extending density functional methods for excited-state systems, but these methods are not so well established as the local density functional methods that can be justified when the material is in its ground state.


Photoexcited states of molecules or materials are often studied with “pump-probe” laser techniques, in which a short optical pulse excites the ground state, and a second probe pulse interrogates the electronic excited state. These measurements are facilitated by the abundance of monochromatic photons from laboratory lasers. More recently, it seems that pulsed synchrotron x-ray beams are emerging as the probe of choice for measuring the atomic structures of excited materials. Neutrons, on the other hand, promise significant advantages for measuring dynamics. Fortunately, the short pulses from spallation neutron sources are well matched to the lifetimes of excitations in many materials. The development of high-power pulsed neutron sources and efficient instruments at the SNS creates the opportunity to use neutron beams to probe the dynamics of excited materials. With the time-resolved Raman spectrometer system being built at Caltech, we plan to lay the groundwork for the development of pump-probe neutron inelastic scattering experiments on instruments such as ARCS at the SNS.


New methods of materials synthesis may be possible with the altered thermodynamics and kinetics of materials in excited states. The possibility of converting graphite to diamond by electronic excitations has been discussed extensively. It is early to promise future applications from basic research on excited materials, but this field does address issues at the core of systems for photocatalytic production of hydrogen, and the design of new materials with a controlled response to ionizing radiation, for example.
