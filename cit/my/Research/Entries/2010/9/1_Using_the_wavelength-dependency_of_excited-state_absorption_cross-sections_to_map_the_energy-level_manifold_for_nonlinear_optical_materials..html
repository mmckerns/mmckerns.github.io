The nonlinear optical (NLO) responses of solutions of metallotexaphyrin (MTXP) complexes at 532 nm indicate that these materials have great promise as excited-state absorbing optical materials, although their full NLO characterization is only in its infancy. Degenerate four wave mixing (DFWM) experiments suggest that MTXP complexes have a predominantly electronic nonlinearity at 532 nm, and simulations of optical limiting (OL) measurements demonstrate reverse-saturable absorption (RSA) for MTXP complexes due to strong excited-state absorption (ESA) at 532 nm. For applications such as optical switches, it is desirable to design OL materials that exhibit strong RSA. However, material parameters such as absorption cross-section are wavelength-dependent; therefore, to fully understand the role of these parameters in the ESA and NLO response of MTXP complexes, measurements at a wide range of wavelengths must be performed. Most measurements of nonlinear response of MTXP complexes, however, have been performed at a single wavelength (532 nm) due in large part to the limitations of modern laser technology. To obtain a fluence strong enough to sufficiently characterize the NLO responses of solutions of MTXP complexes, a relatively strong laser such as an Nd:YAG must be used as the light source. The use of an Nd:YAG as a light source constrains the available wavelengths to 1064 nm, 532 nm, and harmonics thereof and, thus, only provides for a very slight set from which to draw conclusions. To allow for a more complete study of the NLO responses of MTXP complexes, a new, powerful, continuously tunable (over 0.85-1.05 µm and harmonics thereof) laser system has been constructed utilizing LiF:F2+** color centers as the active medium.

 

Using the wavelength-dependency of excited-state absorption cross-sections to map the energy-level manifold for nonlinear optical materials.

September 1, 2010

Seven-state energy-level model of nonlinear absorption. Each of the electronic energy levels has a manifold of vibrational and rotational sublevels associated with it; however, for metalorganic reverse saturable absorbing molecules these sublevels have negligible effect. Single- and two-photon absorption cross sections [sij and sik(2)] or decay rates [kji] are representative of the labeled energy-level transition. Large intersystem crossings, coupled with long-lived triplet excited states and large excited-state absorption cross-sections are commonly very desirable in the design of efficient nonlinear optical devices.

We have designed and built a high-powered tunable laser system that operates at energies capable of measuring a NLO response from strongly nonlinear optical materials, such as MTXP complexes. The pumping beam originated from a frequency-doubled, Q-switched Nd:YAG laser operating at a laser pulse repetition rate of 10 Hz with 5 ns pulses and an energy per pulse of up to 640 mJ and was subsequently Raman shifted in a deuterium cell using a backscattering configuration to provide light at 633 nm and a maximum energy per pulse of 298 mJ. To increase lasing efficency of the LiF:F2+** crystal, the incident beam was threefold compressed prior to entering the cavity by a telescope. The use of two sets of different cavity mirrors was necessary to cover the whole range of tunability for the LiF:F2+** crystal. Furthermore, a plane mirror that reflected greater than 99% at 633 nm was stacked on the back end of the 0° cavity mirror to provide for second-pass pumping of the LiF:F2+** crystal. The reflected beam was set incident upon a plane diffraction grating with 1200 groves/nm in a Littrow configuration, where the efficiency of diffraction of the first order was then measured to be ~50%. A 15 cm focal length lens was inserted in the cavity at 5 cm from the grating to aid in mode suppression and output beam profile. These modifications pushed the total LiF:F2+** cavity size to about 15 cm. The zero-order diffraction output produced at the grating was reflected off an orthogonally mounted broadband reflective mirror, such that frequency tuning was produced by rotating the grating/mirror corner reflector about the axis perpendicular to the plane of grating dispersion, thus providing unchanged direction of propagation for the output beam.


Output lasing was found to be maximal at 937 nm and to have a line width (FWHM) of less than 1.5 nm across the full tuning range. At this wavelength, the laser provides a maximum output average power of 69 mW, achieved with 700 mW of input power incident on the crystal at 633 nm (yielding a real efficiency of ~10% and a lasing threshold of 17.4 mJ/cm2). This range of input power was found to be at the damage threshold (~4.5 J/cm2) for the 45° input cavity mirror. To avoid damaging the input mirror, the tuning curve for the LiF:F2+** resonator was measured for input pumping of 633 nm at a power of 385 mW. The LiF:F2+** laser recorded output power to 92 mW (at 950 nm), energy per pulse of 9.2 mJ, and ~15% real efficiency under 620 mW of pumping at 633 nm and is further expected to perform more efficiently at higher fluences than linear scaling would provide; that is, output power should be greater than 442 mW for 633 nm pumping at 2.98 W. Tunability across a range of 850-1060 nm was recorded; furthermore, utilizing second harmonic generation from a single BBO crystal extended the range to cover 425-530 nm, as well.

(Left) Experimental setup for the LiF:F2+** color center laser. Plots of conversion efficiency for the wavelength range and cavity losses for an output coupler of R = 90% demonstrate the tunability and efficiency for the new laser. (Right) Transmission versus incident fluence at 5 ns for four concentrations of chloroform solution of [CH3– CdTXP]Cl at two different wavelengths. The extension of the measured fluence to greater than 1 J/cm2 compelled the addition of higher-order triplet transitions to permit more-accurate modeling of chloroform-solvated [CH3– CdTXP]Cl at practical optical device energies. The delay in nonlinearity at 633 nm in the 5 ns transmission response for [CH3-CdTXP]Cl may be due to the triplet-triplet absorption minimum at that wavelength.


Dynamics of population density for a seven-state energy-level model of nonlinear absorption at 5 ns and 532 nm for a 4.33 x 10-4 mol/L methanol solution of [C6H4– CdTXP]Cl. Normalized incident pulse intensity (Iin) and normalized population density are shown for each electronic energy level and for four input fluences Fin. Population dynamics simulations also provided fitted values for absorption cross sections and lifetimes of the excited states. The fitted cross sections were used to calculate the dynamics of absorbed energy Eij = sijNiI for each of the four input fluences. At high-fluences, the second excited triplet state was shown to play a larger role than expected.

OL and transmittance curves for [CH3-CdTXP]Cl in chloroform were obtained for 5 ns pulses at 452, 467, 475, 500, 515, 532, and 633 nm. Measurements at 532 and 633 nm were made with a variably attenuated input beam across energies spanning to just over 1 J. Similarly, measurements at 452, 467, 475, 500, and 515 nm utilized input beam energies of slightly less than 10 mJ, where the frequency-doubled output from the LiF:F2+** laser was generated utilizing fluences just under the damage threshold for the input cavity mirror.


The use of the LiF:F2+** laser as a source for DFWM, Z-scan, and OL measurements will allow for unprecedented characterization of the NLO response of materials.  A tool allowing for a full characterization of the NLO response due to ESA for MTXP complexes has been constructed; in conjunction, a program has been written to allow for determination of the nanosecond transmission behavior and population dynamics at a wide range of wavelengths. The absorption cross-sections for each selected wavelength can be calculated and the contribution of each energy level to the NLO response of the medium can be determined. Further development of the LiF:F2+** laser and nonlinear absorption measurement system to provide for a higher level of input fluence for the OL measurements should allow the measurement and determination of the higher excited-state parameters of the MTXP solutions. The coupling of the tunable laser with the semiempirical model for ESA potentially provides not only an understanding of the wavelength-dependent nature of the fitted absorption cross-sections but, under careful analysis of the change in the transmission characteristics with the change in wavelength, an accurate map of the energy-level manifold of the [CH3-CdTXP]Cl solution. This mapping of the energy levels should be a viable alternative (or complement) to performing extremely difficult triplet and singlet excited-state spectroscopy. Furthermore, the effects of dispersion and resonance on third-order nonlinear susceptibility can be measured through wavelength-tunable DFWM and Z-scan studies.
